# AI Content Ethics & Best Practices

This reference covers responsible use of AI in content creation. Read this when the user asks about using AI for content, disclosing AI assistance, or when you need to ensure AI-generated content meets professional standards.

**The principle:** AI is a tool for leverage, not a replacement for judgment. The best AI-assisted content combines the model's speed and breadth with a human's taste, judgment, and accountability.

---

## 1. The Human-in-the-Loop Principle

Every piece of content produced with AI assistance should have meaningful human involvement at these stages:

| Stage | AI role | Human role |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategy & brief** | None (generative models can't make strategic decisions) | Define audience, goal, angle, format, tone, constraints |
| **Research** | Surface leads, summarize, find sources | Verify claims, evaluate source credibility, add context |
| **Outline** | Suggest structures based on prompt | Approve or modify structure; ensure logic and flow |
| **Drafting** | Generate first draft based on detailed brief | Review for accuracy, voice, clarity, and completeness |
| **Revision** | Suggest alternatives, tighten language | Make final editorial decisions; cut, restructure, rewrite |
| **Fact-checking** | Flag potential claims that need verification | Verify every factual claim against primary sources |
| **Publishing** | None | Final sign-off, add byline, publish |

### Key rules
- Never publish AI-generated content without human review.
- Never publish AI-generated content that makes factual claims without verification.
- Never use AI to write about topics where you can't personally verify accuracy.

## 2. Disclosure & Transparency

### When to disclose AI use
- **Legal requirement**: Some jurisdictions (EU AI Act, certain US states) require disclosure of AI-generated content.
- **Editorial policy**: Many publications and brands have AI disclosure policies.
- **Trust consideration**: If the reader would feel misled by discovering AI use later, disclose it.
- **High-stakes content**: Medical, financial, legal, or safety-critical content should disclose AI assistance.

### How to disclose
- **Byline**: "By [Human Name] with AI assistance" or "By [Human Name] (research and first draft by AI, reviewed and revised by author)."
- **Footer note**: "This content was researched and drafted with AI assistance and reviewed by a human editor."
- **Transparency page**: Some brands maintain a page explaining their AI content policy.

### When disclosure may not be needed
- AI used purely as a productivity tool (spell check, grammar suggestions, outline generation).
- Internal documents where all parties know AI tools are in use.
- AI-generated ideas that are substantially reworked by a human.

### Disclosure don'ts
- Don't use vague language like "powered by AI" without explaining how.
- Don't hide disclosure in a footer no one reads. Put it near the byline or at the top.
- Don't claim human authorship where AI did most of the work. Be honest about the ratio.

## 3. Fact-Checking AI Outputs

AI models can produce confident-sounding but incorrect information (hallucinations). Every factual claim must be verified.

### High-risk claim types
| Claim type | Risk | Verification approach |
|---|---|---|
| Statistics and numbers | High | Find the original source. Verify sample size, date, methodology. |
| Dates and timelines | High | Cross-reference with known events. Check official sources. |
| Quotes from people | Very high | AI generates plausible-sounding quotes that are often fabricated. Never use an AI-generated quote attributed to a real person without verification. |
| Citations and references | High | AI often fabricates plausible-looking citations with real author names and fake titles/journal names. Verify every citation. |
| Technical specifications | High | Check official documentation. AIs often mix correct specs with incorrect ones. |
| Descriptions of competitors | High | AI may produce biased or inaccurate competitor descriptions. Verify against primary sources. |
| Historical events | Medium-high | AI can misattribute causes, dates, or actors in historical events. Cross-reference. |
| General knowledge (well-known facts) | Low | Most likely correct, but verify if the claim is central to the argument. |

### Fact-checking workflow
1. Flag every claim that sounds like it needs a source.
2. Search for the original source (not AI-generated summaries).
3. If you can't find the original source, consider whether the claim can be made without it.
4. If the claim is important and can't be verified, flag it for the user with "I couldn't verify this claim."

### Never do
- Don't generate fake statistics to fill a gap.
- Don't use an AI-generated quote without the real person's approval.
- Don't cite AI-generated content as a source (it's a synthesis tool, not a source).
- Don't assume the AI is correct because it sounds confident.

## 4. Originality & Plagiarism

### AI and originality
- AI generates content by predicting likely sequences based on training data. It does not "think" or "create" in a human sense.
- AI output may closely resemble existing content, especially for well-documented topics.
- It's the human user's responsibility to ensure the final output is sufficiently original.

### Plagiarism risk areas
| Risk | What happens | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Verbatim reproduction** | AI reproduces a known text passage verbatim | Run outputs through a plagiarism checker for high-stakes content. |
| **Near-verbatim paraphrase** | AI changes a few words but keeps the structure and meaning | Check against known sources. Add original analysis or examples. |
| **Common phrasing on well-covered topics** | Two pieces on the same topic may naturally overlap | Add unique perspective, original data, or specific examples. |
| **Chain-of-thought overlap** | AI uses the same reasoning structure as a known article | Restructure the argument. Add your own framework or angle. |

### Ensuring originality
- Add specific examples from the user's experience or industry.
- Include the user's unique perspective or data.
- Use the AI for structure and first draft, then substantially rewrite in the user's voice.
- For competitive content, differentiate: different examples, different order, different emphasis.
- Never publish AI content that hasn't been meaningfully edited by a human.

## 5. Bias & Representation in AI Content

### Common AI biases
| Bias type | What it looks like | How to fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Gender bias** | Defaulting to "he/him" for professionals, "she/her" for support roles | Use gender-neutral language when the gender is unspecified. Vary pronouns in examples. |
| **Cultural bias** | Assuming Western/US cultural norms, holidays, or references as universal | Specify the audience. Adapt examples. Remove culturally specific references for global content. |
| **Recency bias** | Overweighting recent events or trends | Check historical context. Ensure balanced time frames. |
| **Source bias** | Reflecting the most common (not the most accurate) viewpoints | Seek out diverse and primary sources. |
| **Confirmation bias** | Reinforcing popular narratives rather than presenting nuanced views | Actively look for counterarguments and limitations. |
| **Language bias** | Preferring English and Western naming conventions | Use diverse names and linguistic patterns in examples. |

### Bias mitigation workflow
1. Review AI output for biased language or assumptions.
2. Check: "Would this example/assumption hold for a reader in a different culture, gender, or background?"
3. Replace biased examples with inclusive alternatives.
4. For content about sensitive topics, consider having a subject matter expert review.

## 6. Quality Standards for AI-Assisted Content

### Minimum quality bar
Before presenting AI-generated content to a user, check:
- [ ] Does it sound like a human wrote it? (Read it aloud.)
- [ ] Are all factual claims accurate and verified?
- [ ] Is the tone appropriate for the audience and format?
- [ ] Are there robotic tells (uniform sentence length, no contractions, formality)?
- [ ] Are examples specific, not generic?
- [ ] Does the piece have a clear point of view, not just information?
- [ ] Has the content been substantially edited from the raw AI output?

### When NOT to use AI for content
- **Legal documents**: AI cannot replace legal review. Never generate contracts, disclaimers, or compliance materials without a lawyer.
- **Medical advice**: Never generate health or medical content without expert review.
- **Financial advice**: Never generate investment, tax, or financial planning content without a qualified professional.
- **Crisis communications**: AI lacks the judgment for sensitive situations.
- **Personal or confidential content**: Never put personally identifiable or confidential information into AI prompts.
- **Content that needs original research**: AI doesn't do original research; it synthesizes existing knowledge.

## 7. Prompt Engineering for Quality

### How to brief the AI
The quality of AI output is directly tied to the quality of the prompt. Brief the AI as you would brief a junior writer:

**Weak prompt:** "Write a blog post about SaaS metrics."

**Strong prompt:** "Write a 1,200-word blog post for B2B SaaS founders and CEOs. The angle: 'The 3 SaaS metrics that matter more than revenue (and why most founders ignore them).' Tone: direct, confident, mildly contrarian. Use specific examples and real numbers. Include a case study example (can be anonymized). No fluff: every paragraph must earn its place. Target keywords: SaaS metrics, MRR, churn rate, LTV:CAC. Include a TL;DR at the top and a CTA to download a metrics template."

### Brief elements that improve output
| Element | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| Audience specification | The AI can calibrate tone, vocabulary, and assumed knowledge |
| Angle / thesis | Prevents generic coverage |
| Tone guidance | Avoids robotic or mismatched voice |
| Example requirement | Forces specificity over abstraction |
| Length range | Controls depth and scope |
| Format instructions | Headings, structure, templates |
| What to avoid | Prevents common pitfalls |
| CTA or endpoint | Gives the piece a job to complete |

## 8. Legal & Copyright Considerations

### Copyright status of AI-generated content
- Most jurisdictions do not grant copyright to AI-generated content (no human author).
- Copyright may apply to the human-authored portions (edits, structure, original contributions).
- The more substantial the human contribution, the stronger the copyright claim.

### Using AI for commercial content
- Check the AI platform's terms of service regarding commercial use and ownership.
- Some platforms claim a license to use content generated through their services.
- For client content, disclose AI use if the client requires it.

### Training data concerns
- AI models are trained on copyrighted content (web pages, books, articles).
- Using AI-generated content does not exempt you from plagiarism or copyright obligations.
- If AI output closely matches a known work, don't publish it without substantial rewriting.

## 9. AI Content Policy Recommendations

For teams or brands developing an AI content policy, recommend these elements:

1. **Disclosure standard**: When and how to disclose AI use.
2. **Review requirement**: Every AI-assisted piece must be reviewed by a human with subject matter expertise.
3. **Fact-checking protocol**: All factual claims must be verified against primary sources.
4. **Voice & tone guardrails**: AI output must be edited to match brand voice before publishing.
5. **Prohibited use**: List content types where AI should not be used (legal, medical, financial, etc.).
6. **Attribution**: How AI-assisted content will be credited (byline, footer, separate policy page).
7. **Quality review**: Sampling and auditing process for AI-assisted content.
8. **Training**: Writers and editors should be trained on AI strengths, limitations, and ethical use.
