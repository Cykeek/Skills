# Design Thinking — Principles, Processes & Philosophy

A reference of foundational design principles, modern operating models, and practical wisdom from influential thinkers in product design. Use this when you need philosophical grounding, framing methods, critique models, or guidance on how experienced designers approach ambiguity.

---

## 1. Dieter Rams — 10 Principles of Good Design

**Who he is:** Legendary industrial designer for Braun. His principles heavily influenced Apple's design philosophy and remain one of the clearest frameworks for evaluating design quality.

**The core philosophy:** Design is not decoration or self-expression. It is disciplined problem-solving in service of usefulness, clarity, and honesty.

### The 10 Principles

| # | Principle | What it means for product design |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Good design is innovative** | Innovation should improve use, not just look new. |
| 2 | **Good design is useful** | If the product does not solve a real problem, polish is irrelevant. |
| 3 | **Good design is aesthetic** | Care in form signals care in function and quality. |
| 4 | **Good design is understandable** | The user should intuit how it works. Self-evident beats explained. |
| 5 | **Good design is unobtrusive** | The interface should support the user's task, not compete for attention. |
| 6 | **Good design is honest** | No dark patterns, false urgency, fake scarcity, or misleading defaults. |
| 7 | **Good design is long-lasting** | Avoid trend-following that quickly becomes noise. |
| 8 | **Good design is thorough** | Details, states, and edge cases deserve the same care as the hero screen. |
| 9 | **Good design is environmentally friendly** | In digital: respect time, attention, battery, bandwidth, and cognitive load. |
| 10 | **Good design is as little design as possible** | "Less, but better." Remove everything that does not earn its place. |

### How to apply as a designer
- Ask of every element: useful, understandable, honest, or removable?
- Use Principle 6 as a line you do not cross in growth, billing, and consent flows.
- If the design needs instructions to be usable, return to Principle 4.

## 2. Steve Krug — Usability & "Don't Make Me Think"

**Who he is:** Author of *Don't Make Me Think* and one of the clearest voices in practical usability.

**The core philosophy:** A product should be so self-evident that the user does not need to stop and decode what to do.

### The #1 Rule: Don't Make Me Think!
A screen should be obvious enough that a first-time user understands the main action with minimal effort.

### People Scan and Satisfice
Users do not read carefully. They scan, latch onto the first reasonable path, and move on.

Design for this reality:
- clear hierarchy
- strong conventions
- low ambiguity
- short, useful copy

### Omit Needless Words
Every unnecessary word weakens the meaningful ones. Remove welcome blurbs, filler labels, and defensive instructions.

### Test Early, Test Often
Three to five users regularly will usually teach you more than a large, infrequent formal study.

### How to apply as a designer
- Define one obvious primary action per screen.
- If a user needs explanatory text to understand where to click, redesign first.
- If a flow feels cluttered, cut words before adding visuals.

## 3. Don Norman — Human-Centered Design

**Who he is:** Cognitive scientist, co-founder of Nielsen Norman Group, and author of *The Design of Everyday Things*.

**The core philosophy:** Design for people as they actually are: distracted, emotional, fallible, and constrained.

### Core ideas

#### Affordances and signifiers
- **Affordance** — What an object can do
- **Signifier** — The cue that tells the user what can be done

A design fails if users must guess which elements are interactive.

#### Mental models
People bring expectations from prior experiences. Align with those expectations when possible, and be consistent when introducing new patterns.

#### Feedback and mapping
- **Feedback** — The system responds in a noticeable, timely way
- **Mapping** — Controls and outcomes feel naturally connected

#### The Gulfs

| Gulf | Question |
|---|---|
| **Gulf of Execution** | Do users know what to do? |
| **Gulf of Evaluation** | Do users know what happened? |

### How to apply as a designer
- Use repeated user errors as a diagnosis of the design, not the user.
- Design feedback for every action, not just the happy path.
- If complexity is necessary, make it learnable and understandable rather than pretending it does not exist.

## 4. John Maeda — The Laws of Simplicity

**Who he is:** Designer, technologist, former MIT professor and RISD president. Author of *The Laws of Simplicity*.

**The core philosophy:** Simplicity is not just subtraction. It is reduction, organization, timing, and meaning.

### The most useful laws

| Law | Meaning | Practical implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Reduce** | Remove what is not essential | Simplify screens, choices, copy, and flows |
| **Organize** | Structure makes complexity feel lighter | Use grouping, hierarchy, and progressive disclosure |
| **Time** | Saving time feels like simplicity | Reduce steps, waits, and re-entry of information |
| **Learn** | Familiarity reduces difficulty | Support onboarding, repetition, and mastery |
| **The One** | Remove the obvious, add the meaningful | Preserve the element that gives clarity or delight |

### How to apply as a designer
- Run a reduction pass before a polish pass.
- Count steps, interruptions, and waiting as design complexity.
- Do not strip all personality in the name of minimalism; keep one meaningful signal if it strengthens trust or emotion.

## 5. IDEO / Tom Kelley — Design Thinking & Creative Confidence

**Who they are:** IDEO popularized design thinking as a human-centered innovation process. Tom Kelley wrote *The Art of Innovation* and *Creative Confidence*.

**The core philosophy:** Innovation comes from empathy, divergence, prototyping, and iteration. The enemy of good ideas is premature judgment.

### The Design Thinking Process

```text
Inspiration → Ideation → Implementation
```

- **Inspiration** — Observe, empathize, frame
- **Ideation** — Generate broadly, defer judgment, explore many options
- **Implementation** — Prototype, test, refine, repeat

### IDEO's 7 brainstorming rules
1. Defer judgment
2. Encourage wild ideas
3. Build on the ideas of others
4. Stay focused on the topic
5. One conversation at a time
6. Be visual
7. Go for quantity

### Creative confidence
Design quality improves when people feel safe to show rough thinking, fail cheaply, and learn visibly.

### How to apply as a designer
- Use brainstorming to widen options, not to choose the final answer.
- Prototype to learn, not to sell a fantasy too early.
- Separate divergence from convergence; doing both at once weakens both.

## 6. Modern Product Design Operating Models

This section captures how experienced product designers work inside real product teams, not just in workshops.

### Shape Up / Ryan Singer — Shape before polish

**Core philosophy:** Good design work begins with shaping the problem and appetite before detailed execution. Fixed time, variable scope is often healthier than fixed scope, variable time.

#### Useful concepts
- **Appetite** — How much time is this worth?
- **Shaping** — Define the problem and a coherent direction without overspecifying details
- **Breadboarding** — Map structure, navigation, and flow without UI decoration
- **Fat-marker sketches** — Sketch big structural ideas that prevent premature polish
- **Thin slices** — Find the smallest coherent version that delivers value and learning

#### How to apply
- Before pixel work, ask: what must be true at the end of this effort?
- If scope grows, cut scope first before sacrificing coherence.
- Use low-detail artifacts when the real risk is flow, structure, or concept.

### Lean UX — learning over deliverables

**Core cycle:** Think → Make → Check → Repeat

Key ideas:
- cross-functional collaboration
- outcome-focused work
- minimum viable UX
- shared understanding over excessive documentation

### Principled critique, not taste-driven critique

A mature critique evaluates work across multiple dimensions:
- **problem fit** — does this solve the right thing?
- **user fit** — does it support the person's context, job, and mental model?
- **interaction fit** — is the flow understandable and efficient?
- **craft fit** — does hierarchy, spacing, copy, and motion support the task?
- **system fit** — is it coherent with the broader product and design system?
- **trust fit** — is it honest, balanced, and safe?
- **evidence fit** — what is observed vs assumed?

Good critique does not ask "Do I like this?" It asks "What user goal, principle, or evidence supports or weakens this decision?"

## 7. JTBD and Progress-Making

Jobs To Be Done is a framing method for understanding what progress a person is trying to make in a specific situation.

### Why JTBD matters
Traditional personas often describe who the user is. JTBD explains why they switch, hesitate, or adopt.

### The job story format

```text
When [situation],
I want to [motivation],
So I can [expected outcome].
```

### Useful distinctions

| Layer | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Functional job** | What the person needs to accomplish practically |
| **Emotional job** | How they want to feel |
| **Social job** | How they want to be perceived |

### Forces of progress

| Force | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Push** | What is frustrating enough to create openness to change |
| **Pull** | What is attractive about the new option |
| **Anxiety** | What creates hesitation or risk |
| **Habit** | What makes the old behavior sticky |

### How to apply as a designer
- Use JTBD when a feature request hides a deeper motivation problem.
- In critique, ask which job each element supports.
- For onboarding, adoption, and churn problems, study the switching moment rather than the current screen alone.

## 8. Responsible and Ethical Design

Experienced designers treat ethics as a design quality issue, not an afterthought.

### Core principles
- **Honesty over manipulation** — no fake urgency, hidden defaults, or coercive copy
- **Clarity over pressure** — make consequences, pricing, permissions, and automation understandable
- **Consent over trickery** — let people make real choices
- **Reversibility over traps** — destructive or high-risk actions need recovery paths
- **Dignity over blame** — do not shame people in errors, billing, or onboarding
- **Proportionality** — ask only for the data, effort, or permissions needed

### Harm review questions
- Who benefits from this design choice?
- Who bears the risk or burden?
- What behavior is the interface trying to pressure?
- Does the user understand what is happening and why?
- Can they reverse, recover, or opt out?
- Would we still ship this if the metric gain disappeared?

### Ethical anti-patterns to flag
- disguised ads or CTAs
- consent flows with unequal visual weight
- misleading free-trial copy
- hard-to-find cancellation
- emotional pressure designed to shame or rush

- AI outputs presented with more confidence than they deserve

### Deceptive & Dark Patterns Auditing

Ensure your designs are free from manipulative structures by conducting a structured audit against the following checklist:

#### 1. Common Deceptive Patterns to Flag
*   **Roach Motel (Hard to Cancel):** Easy to subscribe or opt-in, but requires high friction (e.g., calling customer support, navigating through 5+ page submenus) to opt-out or cancel.
*   **Confirmshaming:** Wording option choices to emotionally manipulate or shame the user into a specific path (e.g., "No thanks, I hate saving money" or "No, I want to keep my site insecure").
*   **Sneak into Basket:** Silently auto-adding secondary items, memberships, or optional warranties to the shopping cart without explicit user selection.
*   **Hidden Costs:** Hiding mandatory service fees, transaction costs, or delivery charges until the absolute final step of a multi-page checkout flow.
*   **Artificial Urgency & Scarcity:** Generating fake countdown timers, vague inventory warnings (e.g., "12 people are looking at this room right now"), or peer-pressure prompts.
*   **Bait and Switch:** Displaying an action button (e.g., a "Download" or "Play" icon) that performs an unexpected, high-risk action like downloading adware or starting a recurring subscription.

#### 2. The Auditing Protocol
1.  **Visual Weight Assessment:** Audit color, scale, and placement of competing choices. If a conversion path is a bright primary button and the alternative option is a tiny, low-contrast, un-clickable-looking gray link, balance them to allow true choice.
2.  **Copywriting Ethics Log:** Scan for double negatives, emotional guilt-tripping, or vague disclosures. Ensure pricing and subscription conditions are expressed in plain language immediately adjacent to the action button.
3.  **Default Settings Check:** Document all pre-checked state inputs. Defaults must represent the safest, lowest-cost, or most data-private option for the user.
4.  **The Fairness Test:** Formulate the core baseline question: *'Would an independent, first-time user describe this interface as transparent, honest, and acting in their own interest?'* If the answer is no, the design fails the audit.

### Deceptive & Dark Patterns Auditing

Deceptive patterns (also known as dark patterns) are user interfaces meticulously crafted to trick users into doing things they might not otherwise do, prioritizing short-term business metrics over long-term customer trust.

#### Taxonomy of Deceptive Patterns to Audit:
1.  **Roach Motel (Subscribed Easily, Trapped on Exit):** An experience that is extremely easy to enter but disproportionately difficult to leave (e.g., subscribing online with one click, but requiring a phone call to customer support during business hours to cancel).
2.  **Sneak into Basket (Hidden Add-ons):** Silently adding an item or service to the user's cart or checklist via pre-selected inputs or hidden pages.
3.  **Confirmshaming (Emotional Manipulation):** Opt-out copy written to emotionally manipulate or shame the user into compliance (e.g., a newsletter opt-out button labeled "No thanks, I prefer paying full price").
4.  **Hidden Costs (Late-stage Surcharges):** Disclosing mandatory fees, service costs, or charges only at the very final step of a checkout sequence, after the user has invested significant time.
5.  **Forced Continuity (Silent Billing):** Charging a customer automatically when a free trial ends without sending a renewal query or providing simple, instant cancellation.
6.  **Disguised Ads:** Presenting advertisements as independent editorial content, organic search listings, or system alerts.
7.  **Misdirection / Visual Dominance:** Using extreme differences in visual weight, color, and positioning to guide users toward an option that benefits the business, while making the user-preferred choice near-invisible.

#### Ethical Auditing Checklist:
*   **The Equal Weight Rule:** Are the "Accept" and "Decline" (or "Subscribe" and "Cancel") actions presented with similar visual prominence and readability?
*   **The Transparency Rule:** Are all dynamic billing models, subscriptions, and recurring payments clearly labeled before the user inputs credit card details?
*   **The Simple Cancellation Rule:** Is canceling a service or deleting an account just as easy as it was to sign up? (e.g., same number of steps, same interface channel).

## 9. Emotional Design and Product Personality

Emotion is part of product quality, but it must support comprehension and trust rather than compete with them.

### Aarron Walter's lens
Functional, reliable, and usable are the baseline. Personality creates attachment and trust when it is authentic and well-calibrated.

### Where emotional design matters most
- onboarding and first impressions
- empty states and first-use moments
- waiting and uncertainty
- error states and recovery
- success moments and reinforcement
- billing, privacy, and high-stakes decisions

### Tone guidelines by moment

| Moment | Best tone |
|---|---|
| **First use** | welcoming, clear, low-pressure |
| **Error / failure** | calm, accountable, recovery-oriented |
| **Success** | affirming but not childish or distracting |
| **Billing / privacy / consent** | plain, honest, precise |
| **High-stakes workflow** | confident, steady, respectful |

### How to apply as a designer
- Define not just what the interface does, but how it should feel.
- Use delight sparingly; reassurance is often more valuable than charm.
- A playful brand tone should never obscure serious consequences.

## 10. Design Processes & Frameworks

### The Double Diamond (British Design Council)

A structured model with two phases of divergence and convergence.

| Phase | Activity | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **Discover** | Research, explore, empathize | Insights, pain points, opportunities |
| **Define** | Synthesize, focus, frame | Problem statement, brief |
| **Develop** | Ideate, prototype, test | Concepts, prototypes, feedback |
| **Deliver** | Refine, build, launch | Final design, specs, handoff |

**Key insight:** Premature convergence is one of the most common design mistakes.

### The Design Sprint (Google Ventures)

A 5-day process for answering critical questions through prototyping and testing.

| Day | Activity | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **Mon** | Understand | Map, target, long-term goal |
| **Tue** | Diverge | Sketches and ideas |
| **Wed** | Decide | Chosen direction and storyboard |
| **Thu** | Prototype | Realistic prototype |
| **Fri** | Test | Learnings and next steps |

### Service blueprinting

When the user experience depends on policy, operations, support, or internal workflows, map both frontstage and backstage.

| Layer | What it includes |
|---|---|
| **User actions** | What the person sees and does |
| **Frontstage** | Product touchpoints, UI, messages, support interactions |
| **Backstage** | Systems, policies, manual ops, approvals, tools |
| **Dependencies** | Teams, vendors, compliance, logistics |

Use service blueprints when the design problem spans more than one screen.

## Quick Reference: Who to Turn To

| Situation | Consult | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Interface feels clunky or confusing | Steve Krug | Fast, practical usability fixes |
| Feature creep or clutter is growing | Dieter Rams / John Maeda | Reduction, hierarchy, and disciplined simplification |
| Users keep making the same error | Don Norman | Mental models, feedback, and human-centered diagnosis |
| Need to generate creative directions | IDEO | Divergence, prototyping, and ideation methods |
| Need to shape a bet before building | Ryan Singer / Shape Up | Appetite, shaping, and thin-slice scope |
| Need to understand adoption, switching, or churn | JTBD | Motivation, anxiety, and progress |
| Need to review a risky or manipulative flow | Responsible design | Trust, clarity, and ethics |
| Need to calibrate tone or personality | Emotional design | Reassurance, confidence, and product voice |
