# DEI Writing Guide

## 1. Inclusive Language Principles

Writing inclusively means recognizing that your audience includes people of different genders, races, ethnicities, abilities, ages, body types, religions, and backgrounds. The goal is to make everyone feel seen, respected, and included.

### Core mindset
- **Person-first by default**: "a person with a disability" not "a disabled person" (though some communities prefer identity-first language, e.g., Deaf community; defer to community preference when known).
- **Avoid othering**: Don't frame any group as "other" or "different" from an implied default.
- **Specificity over euphemism**: Be precise about identities when relevant. Avoid vague euphemisms that obscure meaning.

## 2. Inclusive Language Categories

### Gender & Pronouns
| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| "you guys" | "you all," "team," "folks," "everyone" |
| "chairman" | "chair," "chairperson" |
| "manpower" | "staff," "workforce," "team members" |
| "maternity/paternity leave" | "parental leave" (unless distinguishing for policy) |
| "ladies and gentlemen" | "everyone," "folks," "distinguished guests" |
| "he/his" as default | "they/their" (singular they is grammatically correct) |

- Use singular "they" for hypothetical people: "When a user logs in, they see their dashboard."
- If you know someone's pronouns, use them.
- When uncertain about a group, use gender-neutral terms.

### Race, Ethnicity & Culture
- Capitalize racial and ethnic identifiers (Black, White, Asian, Latino, Indigenous).
- Be specific: "Hmong" not "Asian" when the reference is specific.
- Avoid equating nationality with race ("Chinese" is a nationality, not a racial descriptor).
- Avoid framing groups as monolithic: "people of color" is a useful shorthand but flattens differences. Be specific when relevant.
- When discussing race, name it directly. "Underrepresented groups" is sometimes preferred, but "Black and Latino students" is more actionable.
- Avoid "culturally diverse" as a vague filler. Say what's actually diverse.
- Avoid color metaphors: "whitelist/blacklist" → "allowlist/blocklist," "master/slave" → "primary/replica."

### Disability & Neurodiversity
| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| "crazy," "insane," "lame" | "unexpected," "wild," "intense," "boring" |
| "handicapped" | "accessible" (parking/bathroom), "person with a disability" |
| "suffers from" | "has," "lives with," "is [condition]" |
| "confined to a wheelchair" | "uses a wheelchair" |
| "special needs" | "accessibility needs," "accommodations" |
| "dumb," "blind" as metaphor | be specific: "careless," "unaware," "ignores" |

- Don't treat disability as an exception or afterthought.
- Accessibility documentation should be equally prominent, not hidden in a footer.
- Avoid ableist idioms: "stand up for," "turn a blind eye," "fell on deaf ears."

### Age
| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| "the elderly," "seniors" | "older adults," "people over 65" |
| "youngsters," "kids" (for adults) | specify the age range or generation |
| "boomer" as insult | be specific about what you mean |

- Age discrimination cuts both ways. Don't assume tech literacy by age.
- Age-inclusive examples: show people of various ages in scenarios.

### Socioeconomic Status
- Avoid classist assumptions: "everyone can afford," "simply upgrade to premium."
- When providing pricing or costs, acknowledge that what's "affordable" varies.
- Don't use "low-class," "ghetto," or "trailer trash."

## 3. Representation in Examples

### Do
- Use diverse names across cultures and genders in examples: "Aisha," "Carlos," "Wei," "Jordan," "Pat," "Keisha," "Hiroshi."
- Vary pronouns naturally across examples.
- Include people of different abilities in scenarios (not just disability-focused pieces).
- Show diverse family structures (single parents, same-sex parents, multigenerational).
- Use people in non-stereotyped roles: a woman engineer, a male nurse, a non-binary executive.

### Avoid
- Tokenism: one diverse example in a sea of defaults.
- Stereotypical pairings: "Latina named Maria in customer support."
- Associating specific roles with specific demographics.
- Using diverse examples only in DEI-themed content. Diversity should be Normal in all content.

## 4. Accessibility in Writing

### Readability
- Aim for 8th-grade reading level as a baseline (Flesch-Kincaid). Break down complex ideas, not vocabulary.
- Use short paragraphs (2–4 sentences max).
- Use bullet points for scannability when the content is list-like.
- Avoid justified text: it creates uneven spacing that hurts readability for dyslexic readers.
- Use meaningful headings (H2/H3) that summarize what follows, not cute or vague headings.

### Screen reader considerations
- Write descriptive link text: not "click here" or "read more," but "read the full pricing breakdown."
- Use alt text that describes the content *and function* of images. Decorative images should have empty alt text (alt="").
- Avoid ASCII art or emoji as the only way to convey meaning.
- Use tables only for tabular data, not layout. Include proper table headers.
- Avoid using color alone to convey meaning (add text labels or patterns).

### Cognitive accessibility
- Avoid idioms and metaphors that require cultural knowledge: "hit the ground running" → "start quickly and effectively."
- Use consistent terminology throughout a piece. Don't vary terms for stylistic reasons.
- Write dates clearly: "July 9, 2026" not "07/09/26."
- Avoid negative constructions when possible: "not difficult" → "easy."
- Numbered steps should be truly sequential.

## 5. Cultural Sensitivity

- Research cultural references before using them. An idiom common in one culture may be unknown or offensive in another.
- Be careful with religious references, holidays, and imagery.
- Avoid assuming Western cultural norms as universal.
- When writing for a global audience, avoid:
  - Sports metaphors (baseball, football references don't translate).
  - Food analogies that assume shared experience.
  - Fixed phrases from one language ("faux pas," "ad hoc") unless widely known.
- Use examples from multiple regions and industries, not just U.S.-centric or tech-centric.

## 6. DEI Content Audit Checklist

Before publishing, scan content for:

- [ ] Are pronouns inclusive and correct? (no default "he," no gendered group terms)
- [ ] Are examples diverse in names, roles, and demographics?
- [ ] Is there tokenism? Would this example still be here if it weren't for DEI?
- [ ] Are idioms and metaphors accessible to a global audience?
- [ ] Have we avoided ableist language and disability metaphors?
- [ ] Are color-dependent elements also labeled with text?
- [ ] Is the reading level appropriate for the audience?
- [ ] Are link descriptions clear for screen readers?
- [ ] Is the page hierarchy navigable (proper H1-H2-H3 nesting)?
- [ ] Are call-to-action buttons written as verbs, not generics?
- [ ] Could any phrasing unintentionally stereotype or exclude?
- [ ] Is the tone respectful without being overly cautious or clinical?

---

## 7. When DEI and Clarity Conflict

Sometimes inclusive language is less concise. When that happens:
- **Prioritize clarity** while still being respectful. "Folks" is usually better than "people of all gender identities" in every sentence.
- **Use the community's preferred terms**, even if they're not the most "plain language" option.
- **Err on the side of inclusion** when the audience is broad. For narrow, known audiences, match their norms.
- **Don't performative virtue-signal**, just write well. DEI-conscious writing should not draw attention to itself.
