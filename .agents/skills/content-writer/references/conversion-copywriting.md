# Conversion Copywriting

This reference covers the principles and frameworks for copy that converts browsers into buyers, trial users into customers, and readers into leads. Read this when writing landing pages, pricing pages, signup flows, CTAs, or any content where conversion is the primary goal.

**The principle:** Conversion copy is not manipulation. It's removing every reason to say "not now" and giving the reader a reason to say "yes" that feels authentic to them.

---

## 1. Conversion Psychology Principles

### The 7 conversion drivers
| Driver | What it means | How to use it |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | The reader understands instantly what you offer and why it matters | Headline passes the "5-second test"; value prop is unambiguous |
| **Relevance** | The reader feels "this is for me" | Audience targeting in headline, examples that mirror their context |
| **Urgency** | The reader feels "I should do this now" | Genuine time limits, limited availability, FOMO (only if real) |
| **Anchoring** | The first price/frame sets the reference point | Show higher-tier price first, then the recommended plan as "savings" |
| **Scarcity** | Limited supply increases perceived value | "Only 3 spots left," "Last day at this price" (only if true) |
| **Social proof** | Others' choices reduce decision risk | Logos, testimonials, reviews, customer count, case studies |
| **Risk reversal** | Remove the fear of a bad decision | Guarantees, free trials, "cancel anytime," no-questions-asked refunds |

### The decision friction scale
| Friction level | What the reader is thinking | Copy response |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** (download a template, read a guide) | "This is low risk. Why not?" | Short page, single CTA, minimal proof needed |
| **Medium** (free trial, newsletter signup) | "Will this be worth my time?" | 2-3 benefits, basic social proof, clear value |
| **High** (paid plan, demo request) | "What if I waste money/time?" | Full objection handling, detailed proof, risk reversal, comparison content |

## 2. Conversion Copy Frameworks

### The 4 conversion copy layers
For any conversion-focused page, layer these 4 elements:

```
1. VALUE PROPOSITION
   "What you get and why it matters"
   
2. RELEVANCE SIGNALS
   "This is for someone like you"
   
3. PROOF & TRUST
   "Others like you chose this and succeeded"
   
4. FRICTION REMOVAL
   "It's safe and easy to say yes"
```

### The "So That" chain
Connect features to deeper motivations by asking "so that" recursively:
- "We offer 99.9% uptime [feature] so that you never miss a customer payment [benefit] so that your revenue is predictable [deeper benefit] so that you can grow without worrying about infrastructure [emotional payoff]."

The deeper the chain goes, the more emotionally resonant the copy becomes.

### The "Because" trigger
Robert Cialdini's research shows that adding "because" + a reason increases compliance significantly, even when the reason is weak.

- Weak: "Can I cut in line?"
- Stronger: "Can I cut in line because I'm in a hurry?"
- Copy application: "Start your free trial because you deserve to see what your data is telling you."

## 3. CTA Strategy by Commitment Level

### CTA verb hierarchy
| Commitment | Weak | Strong | Strongest |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low | "Learn More" | "See How It Works" | "Get the Free Template" |
| Medium | "Sign Up" | "Start Your Free Trial" | "Create Your Account" |
| High | "Buy Now" | "Start Your Plan" | "Book a Demo" |

### CTA placement rules
- **Low-friction offers**: CTA in hero and at end of page.
- **Medium-friction offers**: CTA after value prop + 1-2 proof points.
- **High-friction offers**: CTA after all objection handling + full proof.

### Multi-CTA pages
If a page has multiple CTAs, only one should be visually dominant. Secondary CTAs should be clearly lower in hierarchy (text link vs button, smaller size, different color).

### CTA microcopy rules
- Start with a verb.
- Be specific about what happens next.
- Add a risk reducer nearby.
- Test dropping the button label into first person: "Start my free trial" > "Start free trial" in some audiences.

## 4. Objection Handling

### The 3 objection types
| Type | What the reader thinks | Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Emotional** | "I don't trust this / I'm anxious about changing" | Social proof, risk reversal, familiar patterns |
| **Logical** | "I'm not convinced this will work for me" | Evidence, data, case studies, specificity |
| **Technical** | "This won't integrate / comply / scale" | Technical docs, compliance badges, enterprise proof |

### Addressing objections before they're asked
The best conversion copy answers objections before the reader fully forms them. Common objections by page:

| Page | Top objections to answer |
|---|---|
| Pricing page | "Is it worth it? / What if I need a feature that's in a higher tier? / Can I switch?" |
| Landing page | "Is this for me? / Will it work for my use case? / Is it easy to set up?" |
| Signup flow | "Why do you need this info? / How long will this take? / Will I get spammed?" |
| Comparison page | "Are you biased? / What am I giving up if I choose you? / Is switching painful?" |

### The objection-response pattern
```
[Objection restated]: "You might be wondering if this works with your existing stack."
[Acknowledge validity]: "That's a fair concern."
[Evidence]: "We connect with 200+ tools including Salesforce, HubSpot, and Slack."
[Bridge to value]: "Most teams are fully set up in under an hour with zero code changes."
```

## 5. Pricing Page Copy

### Plan name psychology
| Tier naming | What it signals |
|---|---|
| Free / Basic / Pro | Clear hierarchy, widely understood |
| Starter / Growth / Enterprise | Signals company stage / ambition match |
| Bronze / Silver / Gold | Generic ordinal, less meaning |
| Custom names (Hobbyist / Maker / Founder) | Signals audience identity |

### Feature list writing
- Group features into categories (not one long list).
- Lead each category with the strongest feature.
- Use checkmarks for included, text for limits.
- Highlight the plan you want them to buy (usually the middle one).

### Pricing page copy patterns
```
Free plan:
"Get started. No risk. Upgrade when you need more."

Recommended plan:
"Most popular for growing teams. Everything you need to [core outcome]."

Enterprise plan:
"For organizations that need custom security, compliance, and dedicated support."
```

### Risk reversal copy
- "Cancel anytime. No questions asked."
- "No credit card required. Your first report in 5 minutes."
- "30-day money-back guarantee. If you're not seeing results, we'll refund every cent."
- "Pause your subscription anytime. Your data stays safe."

## 6. Social Proof Hierarchy

| Type | Impact level | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Named case study with metrics | Highest | High-consideration pages (pricing, enterprise) |
| Logo grid (known brands) | High | Homepage, landing pages |
| Aggregate numbers ("10,000+ teams") | Medium | Any page, especially as trust signal |
| Testimonial with name + title + photo | Medium-high | Pricing, signup, feature pages |
| Review rating (G2, Capterra) | Medium | Comparison, pricing pages |
| User count / social followers | Low-medium | Brand awareness pages |
| Generic "trusted by..." without specifics | Low | Avoid unless no other proof exists |

### Social proof placement rules
- More ambitious the claim → higher the proof must go.
- More skeptical the audience → more proof types needed.
- Higher the price → more evidence required.

## 7. Trust Signal Placement

| Signal | Best location |
|---|---|
| Logos of known customers | Hero / above fold |
| Security badges (SSL, SOC2) | Signup form, pricing footer |
| Money-back guarantee | Near CTA |
| Customer count | Hero or after benefits |
| Press mentions | Sidebar or between sections |
| "As seen in" logos | Hero or footer |
| Case study links | Near CTA for high-friction offers |
| Review stars | Pricing page, near plan cards |

## 8. Landing Page Conversion Checklist

- [ ] Headline passes the "5-second test": can a visitor understand the offer in 5 seconds?
- [ ] Subhead translates the headline into a clear outcome.
- [ ] One primary CTA. Only one. Everything else is secondary.
- [ ] CTA verb is specific ("Start free trial" not "Submit").
- [ ] Risk reversal visible near the CTA.
- [ ] Social proof visible without scrolling.
- [ ] Objection handling present for the main concern.
- [ ] Page length matches purchase complexity.
- [ ] No jargon the audience doesn't know.
- [ ] Trust signals placed near decision points.
- [ ] Mobile: CTA is thumb-reachable, text is readable without zooming.
- [ ] The page answers: "What is this? Who is it for? Why should I care? What do I do next?"

## 9. A/B Testing Principles for Copy

### What to test
| Element | Impact | Test frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Headline | Very high | Every new page |
| CTA button text | High | Monthly |
| Offer framing (gain vs loss) | High | Per campaign |
| Social proof placement | Medium | Quarterly |
| Risk reversal wording | Medium | Per pricing change |
| Testimonial selection | Medium | Ongoing |
| Page length | Low-medium | Rarely |

### Testing methodology
1. Change one variable at a time (headline OR CTA, not both).
2. Run tests to statistical significance (95% confidence, minimum 100 conversions per variation).
3. Segment results by traffic source (organic may prefer different copy than paid).
4. Don't stop a test early just because it looks like a winner.

### Copy testing quick wins
- Try "loss framing" vs "gain framing": "Don't lose 6 hours a week" vs "Save 6 hours a week."
- Try first-person CTA: "Start my free trial" vs "Start your free trial."
- Try specificity: "Cut reporting time from 6 hours to 30 minutes" vs "Save time on reporting."
- Try social proof format: number ("10,000+ users") vs narrative (case study quote) vs visual (logo grid).

## 10. Conversion Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why it hurts | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Multiple competing CTAs | Decision paralysis | One primary CTA per page |
| Vague value prop | Reader can't tell what they get | Be specific: "Get 3 free templates" not "Get resources" |
| Features before benefits | Reader doesn't care about your architecture | Translate every spec into an outcome |
| No objection handling | Reader leaves with unanswered doubts | Add an objection section before the CTA |
| Generic social proof | "Trusted by thousands" works less than "Used by teams at Google and Stripe" | Name names (with permission) |
| Risk not addressed | Reader thinks "what if I regret this?" | Guarantee, free trial, no-questions refund |
| Too much copy for low-commitment offers | Overwhelms the reader | Match length to decision complexity |
| Too little copy for high-commitment offers | Doesn't answer enough concerns | Long works when every section resolves a question |
| Popup CTAs before value delivered | Reader hasn't earned the ask yet | Give value first, then ask |
| CTA below the fold without a reason to scroll | No one gets there | Give the skimmer a CTA above the fold too |
