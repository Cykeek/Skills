# Website & Dashboard Content Playbook

This reference covers the major content surfaces for a SaaS product: public website pages and in-product dashboard copy. Read this when you need to write or revise homepage, pricing, features pages, or in-app content.

---

## 1. Website Content Strategy

### The 5 essential pages
| Page | Job | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| **Homepage** | Communicate value prop + earn trust in 5-10 seconds | #1 |
| **Features (or Product) page** | Prove the product delivers on the promise | #2 |
| **Pricing page** | Make the decision feel safe and justified | #3 |
| **About / Mission** | Build brand connection (lower priority for early stage) | #4 |
| **Blog / Resources** | Drive organic traffic and nurture leads | Ongoing |

### Additional pages (as the business grows)
- **Comparison pages** (vs competitor X): mid-funnel capture
- **Case studies**: social proof and specific outcomes
- **Docs / Knowledge base**: reduce support load
- **Changelog / Release notes**: transparency and power-user engagement
- **Integrations**: ecosystem credibility
- **Enterprise / Security**: compliance and procurement checklist items
- **Affiliate / Partner**: channel growth

## 2. Homepage Copy

### Structure (top-to-bottom)

| Section | Job | Content length |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | State what you do, for whom, and why it's different | Headline (5-8 words) + Subhead (1-2 sentences) |
| **Social proof** | Prove people trust you | 3-5 customer logos OR a number ("Trusted by 2,000+ teams") |
| **Problem-agitate** | Make the reader feel the pain (skip for well-known problems) | 2-3 sentences |
| **How it works** | Explain the process simply | 3 steps, 1 sentence each |
| **Key benefits (3)** | Why these matter to THIS reader | Heading + 1-2 sentence paragraph per benefit |
| **Objection handling** | Answer the main fear | 1-2 sentences |
| **CTA section** | Drive the next action | Heading + button + risk reversal |

### Headline patterns
| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **What you do + for whom** | "Analytics for product teams who hate dashboards" |
| **Outcome + how** | "Cut reporting time 90% with one click" |
| **The big promise** | "The only tool your support team needs" |
| **Contrarian** | "Most dashboards slow you down. Ours doesn't." |

### Subhead job
If the headline is:
- **Clever** → the subhead must decode it
- **Broad** → the subhead must narrow it (who, what, why)
- **Opinionated** → the subhead must ground it with proof
- **Feature-heavy** → the subhead must translate to outcome

### Social proof placement
- If your brand is new or the promise is bold: put logos high (right after hero).
- If your brand is established: logos can go lower.
- Use the strongest proof first: biggest customer, most impressive number.

## 3. Features / Product Page

### Don't list features. Prove outcomes.

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| "Real-time analytics dashboard" | "See your metrics update as they happen. No refresh button needed." |
| "256-bit encryption" | "Your data is encrypted in transit and at rest. We meet SOC 2 Type II standards." |
| "Integrates with Slack" | "Get a daily summary in Slack. No dashboard login required." |

### Feature page structure
```
HEADLINE: [Outcome, not feature]
↓
[Social proof or number that anchors the page]
↓
Feature 1: [Benefit headline → short paragraph explaining + screenshot]
Feature 2: [Same pattern]
Feature 3: [Same pattern]
↓
[Use case scenarios: "How [audience X] uses this for [outcome Y]"]
↓
[Comparison table vs common alternatives or vs previous state]
↓
[CTA: try it / see a demo]
```

### Feature grid writing (for 3-column grids)
Default pattern:
```
[Short heading: the benefit label]
[1-2 sentence body: the benefit explained with one concrete detail]
```

Use bullets inside a card ONLY when:
- The card is a checklist of specs, steps, or attributes.
- The comparison is feature-by-feature.
- The reader genuinely benefits from scanning discrete items.

## 4. Pricing Page Copy

### The pricing page job
Make the decision feel safe, justified, and low-risk. NOT to explain your product architecture.

### Key copy elements
| Element | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Plan names** | Descriptive over clever (Pro > Premium). Tier names should signal value progression. |
| **Price anchors** | The middle plan should be the default recommendation. Make it visually prominent. |
| **Feature lists** | Group by category. Use checkmarks for included, text for limits. |
| **CTA per plan** | "Start free trial" / "Contact sales" / "Get started" |
| **Objection handlers** | "No credit card required," "Cancel anytime," "All plans include onboarding," "Pause anytime" |
| **ROI calculation** | "Pay $29/month. Save 6 hours/week. That's ~$600/month in time saved." |

### Pricing page copy patterns
> **For the "free" plan:**
> "Get started. No risk. No time limit on your trial."
>
> **For the "pro" plan (recommended):**
> "For growing teams who need more power. Most popular."
>
> **For the "enterprise" plan:**
> "For organizations that need custom security, compliance, and support."

### FAQ section on pricing page
Answer the real questions users ask before buying:
- "What happens when I exceed my plan limits?"
- "Can I switch plans?"
- "Do you offer annual discounts?"
- "Is there a setup fee?"

## 5. Comparison Page Content

### Structure
```
HEADLINE: [X vs Y: which is right for you?]
↓
[TL;DR for the impatient reader]
↓
[At-a-glance feature table: max 6-8 rows]
↓
Deep compare by category:
- Product X strengths
- Product Y strengths
- Where they differ in practice
↓
[Recommendation by use case]
↓
[How we evaluated (methodology note, builds trust)]
```

### Tone rules
- Acknowledge bias: "Full disclosure: we make Product X. We've tried to be fair, but you should read this with our perspective in mind."
- Don't pretend your product wins every category. Readers see through it.
- Compare by use case, not by feature count. "If you need X, go with A. If you need Y, go with B."
- Use the competitor's actual pricing and features. Don't caricature them.

## 6. CRO (Conversion Rate Optimization) for Copy

### CTA placement rules
| Friction level | Where CTA goes | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Low (template, free tool) | Hero + after each section | "Get the template" |
| Medium (free trial, demo) | After value + proof | "Start your free trial" |
| High (sales call, paid plan) | After objection handling + social proof | "Book a demo" |

### CTA verb power
| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| Submit | Send, Get started, Start free trial |
| Learn More | See how it works, Watch the demo |
| Sign Up | Start free, Create your account, Get access |
| Buy Now | Start your plan, Upgrade to Pro |

### Risk reversal patterns
- "Cancel anytime. No questions asked."
- "No credit card required."
- "14-day free trial. Your first report in 5 minutes."
- "100% satisfaction guaranteed. If you don't love it, we'll refund the full amount."

### Social proof types ranked by impact
1. Specific numbers from named customers ("Acme Co cut support response time 65%")
2. Logos of known brands ("Trusted by teams at Google, Stripe, and Airbnb")
3. Aggregate numbers ("2,500+ teams use Acme")
4. Testimonial quotes (real name + title + photo)
5. Review ratings (G2, Capterra, App Store)

## 7. Content Audit Methodology

When the user asks to audit their website content, use this framework:

### Step 1: Audit by page purpose
For each page, ask:
- [ ] What is this page's job? (educate, convert, inform, enable)
- [ ] Does it accomplish that job in under 5 seconds of scanning?
- [ ] Is there one clear CTA?
- [ ] Is the headline specific enough?
- [ ] Does the page answer the reader's main question early?
- [ ] Is there social proof?
- [ ] Is objection handling present?

### Step 2: Audit by user journey
- [ ] Can a first-time visitor understand the product in 10 seconds?
- [ ] Is the path from awareness → consideration → decision clear?
- [ ] Are there content gaps where users get stuck?
- [ ] Are comparison pages present for competitive queries?

### Step 3: Audit by tone & consistency
- [ ] Does the tone match the brand voice consistently across pages?
- [ ] Are there robotic tells (no contractions, formal phrasing)?
- [ ] Is there jargon the audience won't know?
- [ ] Are examples specific or generic?

### Step 4: Prioritize fixes
Rank by impact:
1. Pages that block conversion (pricing, homepage, signup)
2. Pages that lose competitive search traffic (comparison, features)
3. Pages that reduce support load (docs, FAQ)
4. Pages that improve retention (changelog, onboarding)

## 8. Website Launch Content Checklist

- [ ] Homepage with clear value proposition in hero
- [ ] 3-5 customer logos or a trust number visible on homepage
- [ ] At least one case study (can be anonymous)
- [ ] Pricing page with clear plan differentiation
- [ ] Features page organized by outcome, not architecture
- [ ] Blog with 3-5 posts at launch
- [ ] Legal: Privacy policy, Terms of service, Cookie policy
- [ ] 404 page that's useful (search, navigation, not just "oops")
- [ ] SEO: Title tags, meta descriptions, H1 per page
- [ ] Social proof: Testimonials or reviews on key pages
- [ ] Contact / support path visible
- [ ] CTA above the fold on every marketing page

---

## 9. Iterative Improvement Framework

After launch, improve content in this order:
1. **Fix the pages people see most** (homepage → pricing → signup flow)
2. **Fix the pages where people leave most** (high bounce rate pages)
3. **Fix the pages that block the path to conversion** (comparison pages, feature gaps)
4. **Add content for the highest-volume search queries** (blog, guides)
5. **Build topic clusters around the queries you want to own** (pillar pages + cluster content)
6. **Revisit and refresh top pages every 6 months** (update stats, examples, keep current)
