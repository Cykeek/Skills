# Content Frameworks & Decision Trees

This is the deep reference for choosing the right format, length, angle, and structure. Read this when the user asks "what kind of content should I write?" or when you need to pick a format for their goal.

---

## 1. Content Type Taxonomy

| Type | Job | Typical length | Best when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Listicle** | Quick scannable value | 800–1,500 words | Audience wants takeaways fast; topic has clear discrete items |
| **How-to / Tutorial** | Teach a process | 1,000–2,500 words | Search intent is "how do I do X"; process has clear steps |
| **Thought leadership** | Shift perspective | 800–1,500 words | You have a contrarian or expert take; goal is authority, not traffic |
| **Case study** | Prove outcomes | 800–1,500 words | You have a real customer story with metrics |
| **Comparison (X vs Y)** | Help a decision | 1,000–2,000 words | Audience is mid-funnel, comparing options |
| **Definitive guide / Pillar page** | Own a topic | 2,500–5,000+ words | You want topical authority; topic is broad and evergreen |
| **News / Announcement** | Inform quickly | 300–800 words | Timely event; pure information |
| **Opinion / Essay** | Persuade or provoke | 600–1,200 words | You have a stance worth taking |
| **Landing page** | Convert | 150–600 words | Single CTA, single audience, single value prop |
| **Marketing email** | Drive action or nurture | 50–400 words | Audience opted in; goal is click-through or relationship |
| **Press release** | Inform press/public | 400–800 words | Newsworthy event; needs the inverted pyramid |
| **Social post** | Spark engagement | Platform-dependent | Quick value, platform-native format |
| **Video script** | Visual storytelling | 60–90 sec = ~150–225 words | Motion + emotion beats static text |
| **Whitepaper** | Industry authority | 3,000–8,000 words | B2B lead gen; original research or deep framework |
| **Technical doc** | Enable self-service | Variable | Users need to do/reference something with the product |
| **FAQ** | Remove friction | ~50 words per Q | Recurring questions; surface for support load reduction |
| **Comparison/alternatives page** | Capture comparison search | 1,000–2,000 words | Audiences searching "X vs Y" or "alternatives to X" |

## 2. Format Decision Tree

```
What is the user's goal for this content?
│
├── EDUCATE the audience (top-of-funnel, awareness)
│   ├── Quick takeaway needed? → Listicle
│   ├── Process to teach? → How-to / Tutorial
│   ├── Own a broad topic long-term? → Pillar page / Definitive guide
│   └── Shift a perspective? → Thought leadership / Essay
│
├── PERSUADE / Help decide (mid-funnel, consideration)
│   ├── Choosing between options? → Comparison (X vs Y)
│   ├── Looking for alternatives? → Alternatives page
│   └── Validate with a real story? → Case study
│
├── CONVERT (bottom-funnel, action)
│   ├── Single CTA from a specific page? → Landing page
│   ├── Drive a click from inbox? → Marketing email
│   └── Remove buyer objections? → FAQ / Comparison
│
├── INFORM (PR / news)
│   ├── Timely announcement? → Press release
│   └── Internal or update notes? → Announcement email / changelog
│
├── ENGAGE (build audience / brand)
│   ├── Quick value on social? → Social post (platform-native)
│   ├── Moving story / product demo? → Video script
│   └── Showcase expertise / lead gen? → Whitepaper
│
└── ENABLE (post-purchase / self-serve)
    ├── How to use the product? → Technical doc / Tutorial
    └── Answer recurring questions? → FAQ
```

## 3. Length Guidance

Length is a function of the reader's job-to-be-done: never a quota.

### Word count benchmarks (defaults: adjust per request)
| Format | Short | Medium | Long |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blog post | 600 | 1,200 | 2,000+ |
| Landing page | 150 | 350 | 600 |
| Email | 50 | 150 | 400 |
| Social (Twitter/X) |: | 1–2 sentences | a thread |
| Social (LinkedIn) | 150 | 300 | 600 (rare) |
| Press release | 400 | 600 | 800 |
| Case study | 800 | 1,200 | 1,800 |
| Whitepaper | 3,000 | 5,000 | 8,000+ |

### When to go long
- The topic is complex and the reader *wants* depth (technical docs, definitive guides).
- Search competitors are ranking with 2,500+ words on the same query.
- The reader is making a significant investment (case studies, whitepapers).
- The reader is in research mode, not action mode.

### When to go short
- The reader wants a quick answer (FAQ, news).
- The reader is on mobile (social, email preheader).
- The goal is one action, not education (landing pages, CTAs).
- Going long means padding. If you can do it in 400 words, don't write 800.

## 4. Angle Selection

The angle is the *specific take*: not the topic. "Remote work" is a topic. "Why remote work exposes bad management" is an angle.

### The 7 angle archetypes

| Archetype | When it works | Example angle |
|---|---|---|
| **Contrarian** | Conventional wisdom is wrong; you have evidence | "Why OKRs fail most companies (and what to do instead)" |
| **How-it-works** | Process is opaque or misunderstood | "How Stripe actually decides what to build" |
| **First-person** | You lived it; the story is the lesson | "I killed our most-loved feature. Here's what happened." |
| **Data-driven** | You have original numbers others don't | "We analyzed 10,000 SaaS pricing pages. 3 patterns stood out." |
| **Comparison** | Reader is choosing | "Notion vs Confluence: which one for which team?" |
| **Beginner's guide** | Audience is new; no resource exists | "The non-engineer's guide to webhooks" |
| **Myth-buster** | Common myths need correcting | "5 'best practices' that hurt your SEO" |

### Choosing an angle: quick test
1. **What's the one sentence the reader should remember?** That's your angle.
2. **Would anyone else write this?** If the answer is yes, your angle is too generic.
3. **Could a reader repeat this angle at dinner?** If they can't summarize it, it's not sharp enough.

## 5. Headline Formulas That Work

Headlines are 80% of the work. Write 10, pick the best 2, ask the user to choose.

### Proven formulas
- **[Number] [adjective] [things] for [audience]**: "11 underrated tools for solo founders"
- **How to [do thing] without [common pain]**: "How to ship a redesign without losing SEO traffic"
- **Why [counterintuitive claim]**: "Why your best customers are the ones who complain"
- **The [adjective] guide to [topic] (year)**: "The no-bullshit guide to webhooks (2025)"
- **[Familiar thing] is [unexpected comparison]**: "Pricing pages are salespeople in disguise"
- **[Authority/brand] [secret verb] [outcome]**: "How Linear shipped 100 features without burning out the team"
- **[Topic]: [specific promise]**: "Internal docs: how to make people actually read them"

### Headline quality test
- Would this stop a scroll in a feed?
- Does it promise something specific?
- Does it undersell? (Don't overpromise: the piece has to deliver.)
- Is there a curiosity gap without being clickbait?
- Are there strong, concrete nouns and verbs?

## 6. Structure Patterns

### Blog post (the workhorse)
```
[Hook: 1–3 sentences that earn the next paragraph]
↓
[Stakes: why this matters to the reader]
↓
[Body: 3–7 sections with H2s, each with a concrete example]
↓
[Synthesis: the takeaway]
↓
[CTA: what to do next / what to read next]
```

### Listicle
```
[Intro promises the value of the list]
↓
[Items: each with a 1–3 sentence "why": not just a name]
↓
[Closing: the one item that matters most, or how to pick]
```

### Case study
```
[Customer + stakes: who and what was on the line]
↓
[The problem: in the customer's terms, not yours]
↓
[The solution: what you did, why this approach]
↓
[Results: concrete numbers, before/after]
↓
[Quote: in the customer's voice]
↓
[Takeaway / CTA]
```

### Landing page
```
[Headline: value prop in 5–8 words]
↓
[Subhead: what it is, for whom, why it's different]
↓
[Social proof: logos or a number]
↓
[3 benefits: each one sentence + a concrete detail]
↓
[Objection handling: the question they're afraid to ask]
↓
[CTA: verb, not "Submit"]
```

### Comparison page
```
[Quick summary verdict for the impatient]
↓
[At-a-glance feature table]
↓
[Deep section per option: who it's for, pros, cons]
↓
[Recommendation by use case]
↓
[How to choose / next steps]
```

### Press release (inverted pyramid)
```
[Headline: news in 8–12 words]
↓
[Dateline + lede: the whole story in 1–2 sentences]
↓
[Why it matters: the impact in 1 paragraph]
↓
[Quote from exec/customer]
↓
[Supporting details: what, when, where, how]
↓
[About the company (boilerplate)]
↓
[Media contact]
```

## 7. The "Pick the Format" Decision Matrix

When you have multiple valid formats, use this to break the tie:

| If the reader's primary need is… | Lead format |
|---|---|
| "Quickly understand X" | FAQ or listicle |
| "Trust that you can do X" | Case study |
| "Decide between X and Y" | Comparison |
| "Learn how to do X step by step" | Tutorial |
| "Form an opinion on X" | Thought leadership |
| "Buy X right now" | Landing page |
| "Stay informed about X" | News / announcement |
| "See this in motion" | Video script |
| "Reference X repeatedly" | Technical doc |

## 8. Common Pitfalls by Format

- **Listicles:** Items without "why": a name isn't a recommendation.
- **How-tos:** Missing the prerequisite step. State what they need before step 1.
- **Thought leadership:** No original insight. If it could be summarized from 3 existing posts, it's not thought leadership.
- **Case studies:** No metrics. "Improved efficiency" means nothing. "Cut reporting time from 6 hours to 30 minutes a week" means everything.
- **Landing pages:** Too many CTAs. One page, one action.
- **Emails:** Burying the CTA below 4 paragraphs. The CTA should be visible without scrolling.
- **Press releases:** Putting the news in paragraph 3. The first sentence must contain the news.
- **Comparisons:** Pretending your product wins every category. Honest comparisons convert better than obvious sales pitches.

## 9. Reverse-Engineered Structure Patterns

These patterns come from studying strong human-written SaaS blogs, landing pages, comparison pages, and long-form explanatory writing.

### A. Give the reader a fast answer before the long answer
Many strong web pieces are layered like this:

```
[Hook / tension]
↓
[Quick answer / takeaway / verdict / table]
↓
[Deeper explanation]
↓
[Proof / examples / objections]
↓
[Next step / CTA]
```

Use this when the reader is likely to skim first and commit later.

### B. Structure by reader questions, not by internal categories
Weak structure often mirrors how the company thinks. Strong structure mirrors what the reader needs next.

Example order for a comparison page:
1. Which option is better for me?
2. What are the biggest differences?
3. How do price and features compare?
4. Which use case fits each option?
5. What's the recommendation?

### C. One section, one job
Each section should do one clear thing:
- answer one objection
- teach one step
- prove one claim
- compare one dimension
- land one insight

If a section is doing three jobs, split it.

### D. Use contrast to create movement
Strong human-written pieces often move by contrast:
- old way vs new way
- expectation vs reality
- feature vs consequence
- promise vs proof
- abstract claim vs concrete example

When a draft feels flat, add a contrast.

### E. Escalate depth gradually
Don't dump the hardest or most technical material in the first section unless the audience explicitly wants that. Many good pieces move like this:
- recognition
- orientation
- proof
- nuance
- decision / next step

## 10. High-Performing Headline & Subhead Choices

### Headline jobs
A headline should do at least one of these, and often two:
- signal utility
- signal audience fit
- signal a strong point of view
- create curiosity
- reduce the cost of deciding to read

### Common high-performing constructions
- **Utility headline:** "11 Free Templates + How to Make Your Own"
- **Trust-cue comparison:** "Buffer vs. Sprout Social: An Honest Comparison"
- **Opinion-led:** "Code review should be fast"
- **Metaphor-led:** "Meet the night shift"
- **Category + payoff:** "Payments infrastructure that scales globally"

### Subhead rule
If the headline is:
- **clever** → the subhead must decode it
- **broad** → the subhead must narrow it
- **opinionated** → the subhead must ground it
- **technical** → the subhead must translate it into outcomes

## 11. Format-Specific Reverse-Engineered Rules

### Blog posts
- Give the reader an early takeaway or summary if the piece is long.
- Put the most decision-useful insight near the top, not buried in the middle.
- Use named tools, scenarios, or examples every 2–4 sections to keep the piece grounded.
- Close with a synthesis, not just "thanks for reading."

### Comparison pages
- Give a quick verdict for impatient readers.
- Acknowledge bias lightly rather than faking neutrality.
- Compare by use case, not just by feature count.
- Translate differences into consequences: what each difference means in practice.

### Landing pages
- Match the CTA verb to the commitment level the page has earned.
- Put the main objection just below the hero or immediately after benefits.
- Use features as evidence for outcomes, not as the lead story.
- Let page length reflect purchase complexity.
- In feature grids, default to a heading plus short paragraph. Use bullets inside a card only when the card represents steps, specs, or a real checklist.

### Long-form explanatory / narrative pieces
- Start with a pressure point or a person, not a lecture.
- State the thesis early enough that the reader knows why they're here.
- Alternate zoom levels: individual case ↔ system / concept.
- Use the ending shape deliberately: roadmap, warning, callback, or open question.

## 12. Cards, Grids, and Compact UI Copy

Repeated content surfaces need a different writing pattern than long-form sections. For 2x2, 3x3, or larger grids, each card should usually read like a small, complete thought.

### Default pattern

```
[Short heading]
[1-2 sentence supporting text]
```

This reads more naturally than a heading followed by three micro-bullets, and it keeps the grid visually calm.

### Use bullets inside a card only when the card is doing one of these jobs
- showing a checklist
- showing product specs
- listing distinct steps
- comparing discrete attributes

If the card is simply explaining one benefit, one feature, or one idea, prose is usually better.

### Microcopy rules for grids and cards
1. Let the **heading carry the label**.
2. Let the **body paragraph carry the meaning**.
3. Keep each card to **one main claim**.
4. Keep neighboring cards at **roughly parallel length**.
5. Avoid nested structure in small surfaces. A card should not feel like a mini document.

### Before / after

**Too list-heavy inside a small card**

```
Fast setup
- Launch in minutes
- No-code workflows
- Easy onboarding
```

**More human and better suited to a grid**

```
Fast setup
Launch in minutes with a workflow your team can understand on day one. No long onboarding trail.
```
